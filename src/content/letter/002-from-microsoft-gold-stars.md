## The Good Track

I want to be clear about something from the start: I did not leave Microsoft because things were going badly.

I joined Microsoft in 2007 and spent years managing product managers, sitting in rooms where enterprise software decisions got made, and learning how large organizations actually ship things. I earned multiple Gold Star Performance Awards. I was in the High Potential Program — Microsoft's way of flagging the people they wanted to invest in and promote. By every internal metric, I was on a good track.

That matters for the story I'm about to tell, because "person leaves big company to start something" often gets framed as escape. This was not escape. This was a pull, not a push.

## Speaking Developer

Before I managed PMs, I was an IC. I wrote OLE C++ code and worked on database replication. I know what it feels like to stare at a debugger at 11 PM trying to figure out why records are not syncing. That technical foundation shaped everything about how I managed later — I never became the kind of PM leader who waves hands at architecture diagrams and asks "can't we just..." I understood what I was asking developers to build, and they knew it.

That dual fluency — enterprise product management and genuine technical depth — turns out to be exactly what you need when you're trying to build governance tooling for AI agents. But I'm getting ahead of myself.

## What Enterprise Teaches You

There's a particular kind of knowledge you absorb after years inside a company like Microsoft that you cannot get any other way. Not from reading case studies. Not from consulting engagements. Not from blog posts.

You learn how procurement actually works — the six-month cycles, the security review questionnaires, the stakeholder alignment that has to happen before anyone signs anything. You learn what compliance means in practice, not in theory: the difference between checking a box and actually satisfying an auditor. You learn why enterprises move slowly, and you learn that the slowness is often rational, because the cost of getting it wrong at scale is enormous.

You learn that the developers inside those enterprises are smart, capable people who are often frustrated by process — and that the process exists because someone, somewhere, got burned badly enough to create it.

All of this lives in my bones now. And when I look at the AI agent landscape, I see a massive gap where this kind of understanding should be.

## The Gap Nobody Was Filling

Sometime around 2024, I started watching the AI agent space take shape. Not just chatbots — actual autonomous agents that could write code, execute tasks, make decisions. The capabilities were advancing fast. The tooling was advancing fast. The orchestration frameworks were multiplying.

But governance? Compliance? Enterprise readiness?

Crickets.

I saw indie builders shipping incredible agent demos. I saw startups raising rounds on agent platforms. I saw open-source orchestration tools gaining stars on GitHub by the thousands. And almost nobody was asking the questions that any enterprise CISO or compliance officer would ask in the first meeting: Who authorized this agent to act? What audit trail exists? How do we revoke access? What happens when it goes wrong?

These are not hypothetical concerns. These are the questions that determine whether a technology gets adopted inside a Fortune 500 company or gets stuck in a sandbox forever. I have sat in those meetings. I know what kills a deal.

## Seattle, 2025

I should mention geography, because it's part of the story. I'm in Seattle. Microsoft is here. Amazon is here. A dense ecosystem of AI startups is here. The conversations happening in this city about AI agents are some of the most advanced in the world, and I'm in the middle of them.

That proximity matters. Enterprise AI governance is not something you can build in isolation. You need to understand the platforms, the cloud providers, the compliance frameworks, the procurement processes. Being in Seattle means being in the room — or at least adjacent to the room — where those decisions get made.

## AI CRAFTWORKS

So I started AI CRAFTWORKS, LLC. One person. One LLC. Building AgentCraftworks — an enterprise governance platform for AI agents.

I am not a 22-year-old in a garage with a hoodie and a dream. I am a former Microsoft product leader with enterprise scars, compliance knowledge, and the technical background to understand what I'm building. That is not a disadvantage. In this particular problem space, it might be the most important advantage there is.

The product is AgentCraftworks: governance infrastructure that lets enterprises deploy AI agents with the same confidence they deploy human-managed software. Rate limiting, compliance controls, audit trails, identity management for non-human actors. The things that need to exist before any serious enterprise will let agents loose in production.

## The Loneliness Part

I would be dishonest if I did not mention this: leaving a big org for a one-person LLC is lonely.

At Microsoft, I had teams. I had peers. I had the ambient energy of thousands of smart people working on hard problems. I had someone to bounce ideas off at lunch. I had structure.

Now I have a home office, a terminal, and Claude. The days when I'm deep in code and making progress are exhilarating. The days when I'm stuck on a design decision with nobody to whiteboard with are hard. The days when I question whether I'm building the right thing are harder.

But here's what I keep coming back to: the gap is real. The need is real. Nobody with my specific combination of enterprise experience and technical depth is building this thing. If I don't build it, it either doesn't get built or it gets built by someone who has never sat through a SOC 2 audit and does not understand why it matters.

## Growth Mindset Through the Mess

My office has a poster that says "Things are about to get messy." I bought it as a joke but it turned out to be a mission statement.

I'm learning in public. I'm building in public. I have my Azure AI Fundamentals certification and I just completed an Agentic AI Hackathon. I am not pretending to have all the answers. What I have is the right questions, the right context, and the willingness to figure it out in the open.

The transition from enterprise leader to solo founder is messy. The transition from human-centric software governance to agent-aware governance is messy. I'm here for both of those messes, and I'm going to write about them as they happen.
